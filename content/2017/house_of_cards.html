House of Cards¶

Playing with some beer mats in a local pub and building a house out of them, I naturally wondered: how many cards does one need to build a classic card castle \(h\) storeys high?

A classic card castle with four storeys requires 26 cards.¶

Houses with one to four storeys consist of 1, 3, 6 and 10 card triangles, respectively, and they require 2, 7, 15, and 26 cards, respectively.¶

A house of cards can be understood as a number of card triangles, build next to and on top of each other (see figure above):

  • for a house with one storey (\(h = 1\)), we need one triangle: \(t(1) = 1\), with \(t(h)\) the number of triangles as a function of the height of the house \(h\)

  • for a house with two storeys we need three triangles, which can be understood as a house of one storey plus two extra triangles on the side (red triangles)

  • for a house of height \(h\), the number of triangles equals the sum of the triangles of a house of height \(h-1\), plus \(h\) extra triangles on the side (red triangles): \(t(h) = t(h-1) + h, \ \ h \gt 1\)

  • we can thus express the number of triangles as: \(t(h) = 1 + 2 + \dots + h = \frac{h(h + 1)}{2}, \ \ h \ge 1\)

Also note that:

  • ground floor triangles are each build using 2 cards

  • the number of ground floor triangles equals the height of the house \(h\)

  • upper floor triangles (all triangles above the ground floor) each require 3 cards

  • the number of upper floor triangles equals \(t(h) - h = t(h-1)\)

From this follows the total number of cards required \(n(h)\) as the sum of the number of cards required for the ground and upper floors:

\[n(h) = 2 h + 3 t(h-1) = \frac{h(3h + 1)}{2} \sim O(h^2)\]

The total number of cards required grows quadratically. To build a house of cards 10 storeys high we need 155 cards.¶

Extra¶

The total number of cards required \(n(h)\) can also be understood as 3 times the number of triangles (each card triangle requires 3 cards), minus one time the number of ground floor triangles (because those require only 2 cards):

\[n(h) = 3 t(h) - h = \frac{h(3h + 1)}{2}\]

Another way to derive \(n(h)\) is by counting the cards required to reach height \(h\) directly (instead of counting the triangles first), via the same procedure of adding red cards for each additional floor:

\[n(h) = 2 + (2 + 3) + (2 + 2 \cdot 3) + \ldots + (2 + (h-1) \cdot 3) = \frac{h(3h + 1)}{2}\]

The maximum height we can reach with a given number of cards \(n\) is given by (found by inverting \(n(h)\) and retaining the positive solution):

\[h(n) = \frac{- 1 + \sqrt{1 + 24 n}}{6}\]
